## AI is expensive, so why is everyone suddenly trying to use it for all sorts of automation?

If you're following AI at all, you know that the hype is palpable online and in the media these days. And with it comes that magical quality that makes it seem like it's going to solve all the problems, at the click of a button, or a prompt tethered to a monthly subscription in this case.

The problem is that for all its incredible capabilities, AI isn't good at everything. There's a time and a place to use it. This is a lesson that those of us who've ever paid a bill for API tokens have already started to learn. Depending on the complexity of the task and the model, you could quickly end up spending hundreds, or even thousands of dollars on tokens.

The good news is that it doesn't have to be this way. Learning *when* to employ AI is at least as important as knowing how. In this article, I'm going to get into this by breaking down where using AI is, and is not helpful, and try to reframe and restore a sense of sanity around AI automation amid all the hype.

## First, why automation?

Automation, in itself, is a good goal if you want to realize productivity gains and scale your work, but it shouldn't come at a *higher* cost. The purpose of automating tasks is to make you more efficient, and ultimately reduce costs whether that's measured in tokens, work hours or CPU cycles. It's important to approach it in a sustainable way or it kind of defeats the purpose.

Automation is a term that's been thrown around a lot since LLMs hit the mainstream. It's an easy umbrella term for having a machine do your bidding without intervention. But it's also important to understand the distinction between LLMs and deterministic forms of automation. 


## What it is, and what it isn't

What's AI actually good for when it comes to automation? AI is frighteningly good at producing certain types of content, and by exposing it to tools, we can give it the ability to create and build useful things at scale. This definitely *is* a type of automation, and it seems like a miracle compared to the way we did things just a few years ago.

That being said, its output is not consistent. Its performance is based on a changing set of factors that aren't visible to the end user. I mean, none of us *really* know what's going on under the hood, even when it surfaces its rationale and sources as part of a response. 

This fact, plus the dynamic nature of interaction with humans and other machines, means it's adaptable but it might never say or do the same thing exactly the same way. In fact, it's best to assume it won't. LLMs are *probabilistic*, whereas traditional types of automation are *deterministic*. Ultimately, this means that you need to choose the right tool for the job.

## Automation begets automation

The part of this that excites me is that LLMs actually give us the tools to create deterministic automations quickly and easily. In other words, it's a tool that helps you build tools. 

That's really its biggest strength. When I think about vibe coding, I think about coding complex applications. The term brings full-on application development to mind. And although that is a good use case for the technology, it undersells its less complex, and arguably more valuable capabilities. 

You don't need to write huge commercial applications to make an outsized impact on your workflow and productivity. Small automations allow you to use your computer in ways that are only limited by your imagination. And the best part is that once you create these automations you can use them over and over again with or without an LLM.

For example, I use a Mac. This means I have a "nix" based command line along with all of the power that it commands, right under the hood. But even as an enthusiast, I'll admit I'm not that great at creating a useful bash script. I just haven't committed enough time to learn the skills, and honestly I have more important things I'd rather do.

This is where the LLM changes everything. You know what you need to get done, and you have enough tacit knowledge about the tools to describe it. Then you let AI write and test the script for you.

Suddenly, you're running automations that do all sorts of useful things. It's really good at setting up your workspace, and creating new artifacts with specific formatting without ever thinking about it. It just happens every day, automatically. You burned some tokens, and now you have a tool you can use for free for as long as you need it.

### Think globally, act locally

So what do you do next? You ask the LLM to imagine ways that you can leverage your local computer in even more creative ways. Ways that are powered by repeatable, LLM-*generated* automations that happen without tapping into a datacenter to burn CPU cycles. 

Think less about having an agent that can move your files and folders around for you, and more about how it can make actions like these into repeatable productivity loops built on other automation tools. And that's really just scratching the surface now that on-device LLMs are becoming available. 

Strategies like splitting tasks, and then choosing different, more specialized models for each job can save tokens and allow you to get more done because it lets you use multiple work streams at the same time.

For example, you can use your most capable LLM as the supervisor, and let an on-device, open source model like Gemma or Llama handle code generation. These models write the code, and the supervisor LLM or another agent checks its work. 

The code generation doesn't cost you anything because it all happens on device. The upshot is that our computers are likely to get even more powerful, on-device models and when that happens, the sky's the limit.

### So what's next?

In the future, I think we're all going to get a lot better at being specific about the ways we get things done, if for no other reason than to inform our AI so it can build exactly the tools we need for these jobs. Learning to architect good output is going to become the most important thing we'll do as we interact with these things.

These machines are not "thinking" sentient beings with agency, feelings, instinct or motivation. And like every technology that came before them, they absolutely require a human in the loop, and exist to make our lives easier and better.

The novelty and hype may still be in full swing, but the closer you get to this technology, the more you realize what it is and what it isn't. And that makes a huge difference. It transforms AI from a super intelligent alien thing – that wants to destroy the planet, and take all our jobs – into a helpful, efficient tool that we can use to supercharge our innate human capabilities.
