## AI is expensive, so why is everyone suddenly trying to use it for all sorts of automation?

If you're following AI at all, you know that the hype is palpable online and in the media these days. And with it comes that magical quality that makes it seem like it's going to solve all the problems, at the click of a button, or a prompt tethered to a monthly subscription in this case.

The problem is that for all its incredible capabilities, AI isn't good at everything. There's a time and a place to use it. This is a lesson that those of us who've ever paid a bill for API tokens have already started to learn. Depending on the complexity of the task and the model, you could quickly end up spending hundreds, or even thousands of dollars on tokens.

The good news is that it doesn't have to be this way. Learning *when* to employ AI is at least as important as knowing how. In this article, I'm going to get into this by breaking down where using AI is, and is not helpful, and try to reframe and restore a sense of sanity around AI automation amid all the hype.

## First, why automation?

Automation, in itself, is a good goal if you want to realize productivity gains and scale your work, but it shouldn't come at a *higher* cost. Quite the opposite. The purpose of automating tasks is to make you more efficient, and ultimately reduce costs whether that's measured in tokens, work hours or CPU cycles. It's important to approach it in a sustainable way or it kind of defeats the purpose.

Automation is a term that's been thrown around a lot since LLMs first hit the mainstream. It's an easy umbrella term for having a machine do your bidding without intervention. And that's not an inaccurate way to describe it. But it's also important to understand the distinction between LLMs and deterministic forms of automation. 

Once you start framing it that way you realize how different they actually are, and you'll also begin to consider the ways that they complement one another.

## What it is, and what it isn't

First, let's address the elephant in the room. What's AI actually good for when it comes to automation? AI is frighteningly good at producing certain types of content, and by exposing it to tools we can give it the ability to create and build useful things at scale. This *is* a type of automation, and it seems like a miracle compared to the way we did things just a few years ago.

That being said, it's not reliable. It excels at prediction based on an ever-changing set of stimuli that may not even be visible to the end user. I mean, none of us *really* know what's changed under the hood during any LLM update. 

This fact, plus the dynamic nature of interaction with humans and other machines means it's adaptable but it might never say or do the same thing exactly the same way. In fact, it's best to assume it won't. LLMs are *probabilistic*, whereas traditional types of automation are *deterministic*. In a nutshell, this means that you need to choose the right tool for the job.

## Automation begets automation

The part of this that really excites me is that LLMs actually give us the tools to create deterministic automations quickly and easily. In other words, it's a tool that helps you build tools. That's really its biggest strength. When I think about vibe coding, I think about coding applications. The term is a misnomer, because it undersells all its less complex capabilities. 

You don't need to write applications for an OS to make a huge impact on your workflow and productivity. Small automations can allow you to use your computer in ways that are only limited by your imagination. And the best part is that once you create these automations you can use them over and over again with or without an LLM.

For example, I use a Mac. This means I have "nix" based command line along with all of the power that it commands, right under the hood. But even as an enthusiast, I'll admit I can't write a very useful bash script. I just haven't committed enough time to learn the skills required to do something like that, and honestly I have more important things I'd rather do.

This is where leveraging AI changes everything. You know what you need to get done, and you have just enough tacit knowledge about the tools to describe it. Then you let AI write and test the script for you.

Suddenly, you're running daily automations that set up your workspace, and create new artifacts with specific formatting and characteristics without ever thinking about it. It just happens every day, automatically. You burned some tokens, and now you have a tool you can use for free for as long as you need it.

### Think globally, act locally

So what do you do next? You ask the LLM to imagine ways that you can leverage your local computer in ever-more creative ways. Ways that are powered by repeatable, LLM-*generated* automations that happen without the need to tap into a datacenter to burn CPU cycles unnecessarily. 

Think less about having an agent that can move your files and folders around for you, and more about how it can make actions like these into repeatable productivity loops built on other automation tools. And that's really just scratching the surface now that on-device LLMs are becoming available. 

Tactics like splitting tasks into discrete parts and then choosing the right kind of AI for the job can save tokens and allow you to get more done because it lets you use multiple work streams at the same time.

For example, you can use your most capable LLM as the supervisor, and let an on-device, open source model like Gemma or Llama handle code generation. These models write the code, and the supervisor LLM or another agent checks its work. The code generation doesn't cost you anything because it all happens on device.

### So what's next?

In the future, I think we're all going to get a lot better at being specific about the ways we get things done, if for no other reason than to inform AI so it can build exactly the tools we need for these jobs. 

These machines are not "thinking" sentient beings with agency, feelings, instinct or motivation. And like every technology that came before them, they absolutely require a human in the loop, and exist to make our lives easier and better.

The novelty and hype may still be in full swing, but the closer you get to this technology, the more you realize what it is and what it isn't. And that makes a huge difference. It transforms AI from a super intelligent alien thing – that wants to destroy the planet, and take all our jobs – into a helpful, efficient tool that we can use to supercharge our innate human capabilities.
